Enhancing CycleNet for Time Series Forecasting

MILA Institute / Université de Montréal Fall 2024

Abstract

Accurate long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) is critical in various domains, such as energy management, weather prediction, and transportation. CycleNet effectively captures periodicity using its Residual Cycle Forecasting (RCF) technique. However, its reliance on a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) backbone limits its ability to model inter-feature relationships in multivariate datasets. In this work, we address these limitations by introducing two key modifications. First, we replace the MLP backbone with Time-GNN to better capture spatio-temporal dependencies between features. Second, we enhance the model with probabilistic forecasting, enabling it to output distributions rather than point predictions, thus providing confidence intervals for improved interpretability and decision-making. Our experiments demonstrate improvements across several datasets, with Time-GNN enhancing feature dependency modeling and probabilistic forecasting improving both accuracy and interpretability.

Introduction

LTSF plays an important role in various domains, such as energy management, weather prediction, and transportation systems. Accurate forecasting in these areas enables better decision-making, resource allocation, and system optimization by anticipating future patterns and anomalies.

CycleNet is a recent state-of-the-art model for LTSF that excels in modeling periodic patterns. It introduces the RCF technique, which explicitly captures periodic components through learnable recurrent cycles.

Figure 1: Shared daily periodic patterns present in the Electricity dataset.

CycleNet Architecture

The model's key innovation lies in its RCF technique, which decomposes input data into periodic and residual components. The RCF operates in two stages:

  1. Periodic Pattern Modeling: Learns periodic patterns using a learnable recurrent cycle.
  2. Residual Forecasting: Residual components are fed into a backbone model (Linear or MLP) to focus on non-periodic variations.

Figure 2: CycleNet Architecture showing the RCF module and backbone options.

Proposed Modifications

1. Replacing MLP with GNN

CycleNet's original backbone assumes independence between features. GNNs are well-suited for this task as they can explicitly model relationships between features. We replaced the MLP with TimeGNN, a computationally efficient GNN that learns a dynamic graph structure for each sliding window, enabling it to model evolving relationships between channels over time.

Figure 3: CycleNet/TimeGNN Architecture.

2. Probabilistic Forecasting

Point predictions do not capture inherent uncertainty. By predicting distributions (mean and standard deviation), the model provides confidence intervals. We assumed a Gaussian distribution and modified the architecture to output two values for each time step, using Gaussian Negative Log-Likelihood (GaussianNLL) loss for training.

Experimental Results

The proposed modifications to CycleNet demonstrate significant performance gains and comparable results across various multivariate datasets. The quantitative results are summarized in the tables below.

Replacing the MLP with TimeGNN also proved advantageous for capturing spatio-temporal relationships, particularly in the Electricity dataset, where it significantly outperformed the original backbone.

Conclusion

In this work, we enhanced CycleNet by addressing its limitations in multivariate modeling and interpretability. By integrating Time-GNN and a probabilistic framework, we created a more robust forecasting model capable of capturing complex feature dependencies and quantifying predictive uncertainty. These gains were achieved while maintaining the lightweight and efficient nature of the original CycleNet architecture.
